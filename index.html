Introduction

Dr. Jose Protacio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda

(June 19, 1861 - December 30, 1896)

The Philippines' National Hero

Dr. Jose Rizal was a Filipino nationalist, novelist, and key figure in the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonial rule. His writings and martyrdom inspired the revolution that eventually led to Philippine independence.

Rizal's Legacy

Rizal's works, particularly his novels "Noli Me Tangere" and "El Filibusterismo," exposed the abuses of the Spanish colonial government and the Catholic Church, awakening Filipino nationalism. His execution by the Spanish made him a martyr of the revolution.

Biography of Jose Rizal

Early Life

Born on June 19, 1861 in Calamba, Laguna, Rizal was the seventh of eleven children. He showed exceptional intellect from an early age, learning the alphabet at 3 and reading/writing by 5.

Education

Studied at Ateneo Municipal de Manila and University of Santo Tomas before continuing in Europe, earning degrees in medicine and philosophy from universities in Madrid, Paris, and Heidelberg.

Literary Works

Wrote the influential novels "Noli Me Tangere" (1887) and "El Filibusterismo" (1891) that exposed Spanish abuses, along with numerous essays, poems, and articles advocating for reforms.

Martyrdom

Executed by firing squad on December 30, 1896 at Bagumbayan (now Rizal Park) after being convicted of rebellion. His death galvanized the Philippine Revolution.

Family Background

Rizal came from a wealthy family in Calamba, Laguna. His father, Francisco Mercado, was a prosperous farmer while his mother, Teodora Alonso, was highly educated and influential in his development.

The family had Chinese, Japanese, Spanish, Malay, and Negrito ancestry. Rizal's siblings were all accomplished in their respective fields.

European Sojourn

From 1882 to 1887, Rizal traveled extensively in Europe, studying medicine and the humanities. During this time he became involved with the Propaganda Movement, writing articles advocating political reforms.

He lived in Spain, France, Germany, Belgium, England, and the Czech Republic, mastering several languages and absorbing progressive ideas.

The Philippine Revolution

Rizal's Role in the Revolution

While Rizal advocated for peaceful reforms through his writings, his works became the moral foundation for the Philippine Revolution. The revolutionary group Katipunan used Rizal's name as one of their passwords, and his execution made him a martyr for the cause.

Though Rizal himself did not support the violent revolution, believing the Philippines was not ready for independence, his death became the rallying point for the revolutionaries led by Andres Bonifacio.
